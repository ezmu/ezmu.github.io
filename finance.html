📜 Chapter One: "Jevons Paradox" – When Efficiency Eats Itself

What is Jevons Paradox? (A history lesson before we drown in numbers)

In 1865, English economist William Stanley Jevons noticed a strange phenomenon. When James Watt improved steam engine efficiency, coal consumption didn't decrease as expected. Quite the opposite: coal consumption increased significantly. Why? Because the more efficient engine was cheaper to run, which encouraged its use in more factories, more trains, more ships. The result: efficiency didn't reduce consumption; it multiplied it. This phenomenon, known as "Jevons Paradox," is the key to understanding everything happening in the software industry today.

📌 The Final Conclusion (That Will Make Your CFO Cry)

“Software bloat isn't a bug. It's a business model.

- The worse your code, the more AWS servers you need. (AWS wins)
- The worse your code, the more Intel processors you need. (Intel wins)
- The worse your code, the more consultants you need. (Consultants win)

And you? What do you win?
- You lose money (higher cloud bill).
- You lose team time (technical debt).
- You lose customers (slow app).
- You lose your planet (carbon emissions).

Jevons Paradox works against you. Every improvement in hardware efficiency is met with a decline in software efficiency. The result: you run in place, and the faster you go, the more waste you create.”

🛠️ What to Do on Monday Morning

  1. Calculate "technical debt" as a financial number. How much does every hour of delay cost you? Every lost customer?
  2. Run the "2010 Device" test. Run your app on an old laptop with slow internet. If it's slow, your code is the problem.
  3. Audit your SaaS bill. How many licenses do you not use? Cancel everything unused in the last 3 months.
  4. Add a "Performance Budget." Don't release any new feature that increases load time beyond 0.5 seconds.
  5. Share these numbers inside your organization. Awareness begins when executives see the real bill.
